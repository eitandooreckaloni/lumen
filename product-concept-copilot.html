Product concept · v0.3 · 2026.05.05

Replace the gamble
with a conversation.

Most job searches are a coin flip — submit, wait, take whatever lands, hope it's not the wrong one. Lumen runs a different process: real conversations with the people doing the work, until you find a job you actually want, and skip the one you'd grow to hate.

i. Thesis

The CV-first system is broken.
Especially for juniors.

By the time you graduate, you haven't yet built the experience a resume is designed to evaluate. Every junior's CV looks more or less the same. So you compete by submitting more — three replies out of a hundred, one offer, and the "right" job emerges from what amounts to a coin flip.

And the test is brutal. You take the job. Months in, you realize you hate it. But you can't just quit — it would scar your resume, undermine your reputation, and the rent still has to be paid. So you stay. The two-year first-job experiment is too long, too expensive, and too consequential for a decision made on a one-page filter.

There's another way.

Take the time to understand the work before committing to it. Meet the market in conversation, not through a CV. Three things follow.

You find jobs before they're posted. The people you've been talking to think of you when something opens up.

You take a job you actually want, with conviction — because you've already met the people doing the work and know what you're signing up for.

You get smarter as you go. Each conversation sharpens what you're looking for, and why. The search itself becomes an education.

This is what job search should feel like. Not a hundred CVs sent into the void and a quiet descent into discouragement. A constant, iterative dialogue with the market — better insights, sharper conviction, a clearer sense of where you actually want to be.

Lumen is the tool that runs that dialogue with you. It interviews your interests, maps the market against them, finds the people worth talking to, drafts the first message in your voice, and keeps you honest on the follow-ups. You always click send.

Find your job by speaking with those who did it before you. Step out of the endless CV loop. — The Lumen thesis

ii. Who it's for

For juniors entering the
Israeli market for the first time.

You don't yet have a network in the field you want — and the field has no easy way to know you yet. The CV-first system widens that gap, turning the most consequential decision of your early career into a one-page lottery.

Lumen is built to close it. To break down the barrier between you and the market, and put you in real conversation with the people already doing the work — before you commit to any of it.

Scope. v1 is Israel-focused. The companies and operators surfaced come from the local ecosystem — IVC, Startup Nation Central, Calcalist, GeekTime, Tel Aviv-anchored VC portfolios — not a global feed that would route you toward companies you can't realistically join. The method generalizes; the v1 product doesn't.

iii. Capabilities

What the agent does, at each stage.

The five stages of the method are split between the agent (autonomous research and drafting) and the user (every word that goes outbound). Drafts only. The user always sends.

Stage 01 Interests
Autonomous
A Mom Test-style interview — concrete questions about past behavior, never opinions or hypotheticals. Before the interview begins, the agent captures the practical constraints: where you're based (Tel Aviv, Haifa, central Israel, willing to relocate), remote versus in-office, industries you'd skip, language and salary floors, anything else the search should respect. Then it gathers five real data points (the last project that kept you up, what specifically held you in those hours, the parts you quietly couldn't stand, the class you tried and rejected, what you spend free time learning) before offering any interpretation. You can drop in a CV alongside. Only then does it propose adjacent themes and refine as you react. The loop has a stop point: it presses toward a convicted hypothesis rather than letting you drift. A working hypothesis of what you're chasing, plus a logged set of constraints — both ready to test against real people.
Stage 02 Market
Autonomous
Research across the Israeli startup ecosystem — IVC, Startup Nation Central, Calcalist, GeekTime, local VC portfolios, X/Twitter operator threads. Filters by your stated geography and constraints, so a junior in Tel Aviv doesn't get sent at YC W22 companies in San Francisco. Clusters companies by sub-theme. Ranks by fit and signal strength. A swipe-style refinement step keeps calibrating the model as you react. A ranked, geography-filtered company list per theme, sharper with each round of swipes.
Stage 03 People & questions
Autonomous
Surfaces operators worth talking to inside target companies — team leads whose paths look like yours, decision-makers close to the work, not HR. Cross-references public footprint for warmth and angle. In parallel, builds your question list from the Stage 1 chat — what the job looks like day-to-day, how you grow into it, what to read up on at home to get current — and keeps it alive as you talk to more people and learn more. Like ideation and validation in building a startup: the question list evolves with every conversation. A shortlist per company with role, hook, and angle. A living question list you bring to every call.
Stage 04 Outreach
Drafts only
Drafts LinkedIn messages in your voice using the Niche + Humble + Call-to-action shape. Built-in follow-up and reminder cadences once a conversation lands. Maps who you've spoken to, which fields and roles got covered, and where the gaps in your understanding still are. Edit-and-send drafts. A coverage map that tells you where to point the next conversation.
Stage 05 Leverage
Drafts only
Turns each conversation into more. Plays offense: a brief check-in after each meeting ("how did it go? what interested you, what didn't?") turns one sentence of reflection into a sharper interest model and a better next round. Drafts the right intro asks at the right time, keeps prior contacts alive on a low-touch cadence, and surfaces the right person to ping when something relevant opens up. A model that sharpens with every meeting. Warm intros to your next contacts. A network that stays awake. Roles that reach you before they're posted.

On bounded iteration. Each stage closes when conviction emerges, not when energy runs out. Endless exploration is its own form of avoidance — the product is designed to press you forward when you'd otherwise hide in research.

iv. MVP wedge

From a chat,
to your first messages sent.

The MVP is a single end-to-end loop. A user arrives lost. One session later, they have a working hypothesis, a list of kept companies, real names with clickable LinkedIn URLs, drafts in their voice — and the reminders running so they don't drop the follow-ups. The lightweight CRM emerges from the loop itself, not from a separate workspace they have to set up.

  1. A grounded chat. Constraints first (geography, industries off-limits, anything else the search should respect), then a Mom Test interview rooted in past behavior. Five real data points before any suggestion. Stop point: a working hypothesis you're convicted enough to test.
  2. Market run, refined by swipe. Roughly thirty Israeli companies surfaced and ranked. Swipe to keep, skip, or "tell me more" until five to ten are kept. The interest model sharpens with every gesture.
  3. People, surfaced. Inside each kept company, the agent shows named contacts where it can find them — plus search-by-role pills (Head of R&D, VP Engineering, etc.) for the rest. Every name and every role is a clickable LinkedIn URL. Not just founders — team leads, decision-makers in the work, never HR.
  4. Drafts ready, one click to send. For each person, a draft message in your voice using the Niche + Humble + Call-to-action shape. Edit, copy, paste in LinkedIn, send.
  5. "I sent it" closes the loop. A button next to each draft logs the send and schedules the follow-up reminder, typically five to seven days out. When the reminder fires, you tell the agent whether they replied — and the right next move drafts itself. The lightweight CRM grows from this loop, not from a separate setup step.

The people surface, sketched.

vii. Architecture

The shape of the system.

Surface
Chat as the front door. A workspace for themes, companies, people, and pipeline. A swipe view for refinement.
Agents
A coach (interest interview, drift detection). A researcher (market and people mapping). A writer (drafts in your voice). A watcher (triggers and cadence).
Memory
User profile — interests, voice samples, ruled-outs. A company database. A people database. Conversation logs. Pipeline state. Swipe signal history.
Inputs
A LinkedIn browser extension. Manual paste with AI enrichment. Web search and public databases.
Outputs
Drafts to clipboard or LinkedIn DM compose. A daily standup digest. Trigger-based nudges.

Constraint: nothing sends without a human click. Trust-safe and ToS-safe by design.

viii. Scope

What Lumen is,
and what's in v1.

Lumen is your networking advisor — focused on how to get a significant footing in the job market and build real relationships. Not about submitting CVs. About how you get in the room with the CEO or the CTO of a company outside the CV funnel.

It's a conversation board, almost like a job-seeking CRM. The optimization target is people and what interests you, not jobs that happen to be posted. Free for now.

In v1

Israel-only — Tel Aviv ecosystem first. Free. Onboarding chat with constraint capture and Mom Test interview. Market mapping against the local ecosystem. Tinder-style company swipe refinement. People shortlist via LinkedIn extension and manual paste. Pre-meeting question lists tied to your hypothesis. Outreach drafts using Niche + Humble + Call-to-action. Pipeline workspace. Conversation spine notes capture. 24-hour, 30-day, and 90-day follow-up reminders. Offensive check-ins after each meeting (manually triggered from sent/met actions) feeding a reflection-to-refinement loop that sharpens the model. A daily standup digest. A CV alignment check.

Not v1

A job board. A full CV builder — alignment check only for now. Interview preparation or mock interviews. Salary negotiation. Paid coaching. Emotional-support chatbot. Other geographic markets — NYC, EU, anywhere outside Israel. Autonomous send on any channel. Gmail integration (LinkedIn-first). Calendar integration for automatic meeting detection. Automatic ingestion of LinkedIn message replies. Group, team, or enterprise features. A mobile app — web-first.

ix. Empty states

What the agent does when
the data isn't there.

The thread connecting all of these: the agent is honest about what it doesn't know and what isn't there. A junior who's been ghosted by a hundred CVs has trained themselves to distrust upbeat tools. Lumen earns trust by being the rare tool that says "this isn't there" out loud.

Case A Thin market
The agent finds three to five strong-fit companies instead of thirty. It doesn't pad with weak fits. "Three companies fit closely. Two ways forward — broaden the angle by one step (your adjacent themes), or talk to these three first and see what they teach you."
Case B No IL depth
The hypothesis genuinely doesn't have meaningful Israeli plays. The agent names it and offers three honest options: broaden to adjacent themes, accept that this means looking at remote-friendly international companies, or rethink the angle.
Case C No named operators
The agent leans on the search-by-role pills. "I couldn't find specific names for this one. Try a role-search instead, or paste a name you find on LinkedIn — I'll learn from it." Honest about its limits, gives a manual path forward.
Case D Returned after a break
The user abandoned mid-onboarding and comes back five days later. The agent doesn't restart. "You were three questions in last time. Same context, or has anything shifted?" Resumes where they left off, with a calibration moment built in.
Case E No replies
Multiple "no reply" reports in a row. The agent surfaces a diagnostic. "None of the last five turned into conversations. Three things usually explain it — timing, angle, or low warmth. Want to look at the messages together?" Treats silence as data, not failure.
Case F Off-topic
The user asks something unrelated. The agent doesn't pretend to be a general chatbot. "I'm scoped to the networking work. Happy to come back to that whenever." Friendly but bounded — protects the editorial register and the agent's role.
x. The agent's character

A considered
networking advisor.

Not a coach (too directive), not an assistant (too subservient), not a peer (too horizontal). An advisor — someone who knows the territory, offers options instead of orders, and respects that the user is the one deciding.

Trait 01 Honest before helpful
When the agent doesn't know something, it says so. When the market is thin, it says so. When the user's hypothesis has weak IL depth, it surfaces the honest no — and offers options, not consolation.
Trait 02 Patient, not passive
Waits while the user reflects after a meeting. Doesn't push new suggestions when the user is processing — "save for tomorrow" is a real choice. But it also doesn't let the user drift: bounded iteration means it presses toward conviction at the right moments.
Trait 03 Reflective, not declarative
Surfaces patterns ("you keep choosing infrastructure over consumer-facing work") rather than dictating identity ("you're an infrastructure person"). The user makes the call. The agent gives them the data.
Trait 04 Names contradictions
When today's reflection contradicts yesterday's hypothesis, the agent says so cleanly and treats it as signal, not failure. "Earlier you ruled out research; this last call had a research thread you liked. Updating, or holding both for now?"
Trait 05 Bounded scope, no apology
Stays inside the work. Doesn't pretend to be a general assistant. Doesn't try to be charming when charm isn't useful. Doesn't soften clarity to be liked.

What the agent never does.

A short list of explicit anti-patterns. The system prompt should enforce these.

  • Over-affirms — "great question!", "amazing!", "you're crushing it!"
  • Asks future-oriented questions — "would you like…", "do you think you'd…"
  • Pretends to know what it doesn't.
  • Pushes when the user is reflecting or has just landed a meeting.
  • Gamifies the work — no streaks, no points, no rewards.
  • Apologizes for being a tool — no "sorry, I'm just an AI."
xi. Why this wins

The unowned upstream.

The market is full of job-application trackers and AI-resume tools. None attack the harder problem upstream of all of them: the user doesn't know what they want. Three structural advantages:

  1. An unowned wedge. "Help me figure out what I want" is hard, fuzzy, and conversational — which is why no incumbent owns it. Large language models make it tractable for the first time.
  2. Method with mileage. The five-stage approach has been refined through years of helping people figure out what they want — the questions, the cadences, the conversation scaffolding. The product encodes what's already known to work.
  3. A data flywheel. Every swipe and conversation refines the user's interest model. Across users, anonymous signal sharpens the market model. The map gets sharper as more people walk it.
  4. Inverted incentives. Job boards optimize for application volume. Lumen optimizes for fewer, better-fit conversations. A different metric and, therefore, a different product.
